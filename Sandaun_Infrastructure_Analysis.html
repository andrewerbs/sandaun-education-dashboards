What state are Sandaun’s school buildings in?

Across West Sepik — from the Vanimo coast to the Telefomin highlands — the 2025 census counted 2,341 classrooms in 524 schools. Fewer than half are permanent, most need repair, and a quarter are still bush material. The same schools that lack safe rooms also lack clean water and reliable power, and the gaps cluster hardest in Nuku. This dashboard answers four questions for the Board: what are classrooms made of, are they safe, is there water and sanitation, and is there power?

What are classrooms made of?

Of 2,341 classrooms, less than half are permanent buildings. Bush material is not a fringe problem — it is a quarter of all rooms, and in Nuku it has overtaken permanent construction. 127 schools teach entirely (100%) in bush-material rooms, with no durable building at all.

District order: Aitape/Lumi (16%), Vanimo/Green River (23%), Telefomin (31%), Nuku (37%). Nuku has the highest share — bush material exceeds permanent construction there.

Are the buildings safe?

Construction type is only half the story — even permanent rooms decay. Across the province 1,661 classrooms (71%) need repair, and 794 of those (34% of all rooms) need MAJOR repair. At the school level, 196 schools are in critical condition and a further 52 are high-priority for rebuilding works.

Do schools have water and sanitation?

Water is the most acute service gap. 141 schools (27%) have a critical water situation and 128 more are of concern; only 209 rely on a tank (adequate) and just 12 have piped water (good). 102 schools have no source at all and bring water from home. In addition, 241 schools have neither soap nor water for handwashing.

Do schools have power?

Solar is the workhorse of Sandaun’s schools — 190 schools (37%) run on solar, far ahead of generator (47), basic lighting (33) or grid (13). But about 228 schools (44%) have no reliable power: 122 (24%) have none at all, and 106 (21%) returned no information — a data gap large enough that the true figure cannot be confirmed and is treated as a flag in its own right.
