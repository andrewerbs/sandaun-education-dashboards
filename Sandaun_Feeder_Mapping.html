Why is the Grade 2→Grade 3 cliff a mapping problem?

In Sandaun, as across PNG, elementary schools teach Prep–Grade 2 and primary schools teach Grade 3–Grade 8. So the step from Grade 2 to Grade 3 is not just a new grade — for most children it means leaving one school and enrolling at a different one, often further away. If there is no primary school within reach of an elementary school, the child simply stops. The province has only 1.17 primary schools for every elementary school, so many elementary leavers have nowhere obvious to go.

3,699 children lost

Only 70% of Grade 2 children appear in Grade 3 — 12,412 in Grade 2 fall to 8,713 in Grade 3. This single handover is the sharpest loss in the whole system, and it lands at the elementary→primary seam.

How well does each district's elementary cohort feed into local primaries?

The feeder ratio measures Grade 3 intake as a share of the district's Grade 2 cohort, matched to nearby primaries. Below 100% means children are leaving the local catchment without a primary place; above 100% means a district pulls children in from elsewhere. Nuku loses three-quarters of its cohort at the seam; Telefomin pulls children in because families travel to its primary schools.

These feeder ratios differ from the simple grade-to-grade transition (Aitape/Lumi 81%, Nuku 64%, Telefomin 69%, Vanimo/Green River 69%) because the feeder ratio matches each elementary school to the primaries near it. The gap between the two numbers is the share of children who survive the grade step but only by leaving their local catchment.

On the receiving side, are the primary schools catching the children?

There are 216 primary schools doing the receiving. Each is classified by how its Grade 3 intake compares to the Grade 2 cohort nearby. The dominant finding is that 126 primaries have no matchable feeder data at all — we cannot yet say which elementary school feeds them. Of those we can match, 46 are losing many children (nearby Grade 2 far exceeds their Grade 3 intake) and only 32 absorb their full local cohort.

What should the Board do about the handover?

Three moves close the seam between elementary and primary, in order of leverage.

Map every elementary to a primary

Resolve the 126 unmatched primaries first. Assign each of the province's elementary schools a named destination primary so the Board can distinguish real dropout from unrecorded transfer, and see exactly where the feeder links break.

Site new primary capacity in Nuku

A 24% feeder ratio means most Nuku children have no primary place within reach. Prioritise new or upgraded Grade 3–8 capacity in Nuku catchments where many elementary leavers have nowhere to go.

Transport and boarding to close distance

Where capacity sits in hub primaries — as Telefomin's 128% shows — close the distance gap with feeder transport or boarding so a child does not stop at Grade 2 simply because the primary is too far.
