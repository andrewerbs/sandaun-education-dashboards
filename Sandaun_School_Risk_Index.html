What this index tells the Board

This index turns the 2025 census into a single, comparable score so the Board can decide where to send people and money first. Each of 521 scored schools (520 fully matched to infrastructure) carries between zero and six risk flags. The higher the score, the more ways a school is failing its children at once — and the stronger the case for an immediate visit.

How is the index built?

Every school is checked against six simple, binary tests. Each test it fails adds one point. A school can score from 0 (passes all six) to 6 (fails all six). The flags are deliberately basic — they describe whether a school can teach safely at all, not how well it teaches.

1 · Teacher gapMore than 40 pupils per teacher, or children enrolled with zero teachers posted.
2 · No usable toiletThe school has no working toilet for its children.
3 · Critical waterWater is bring-from-home, none at all, or missing from the return.
4 · No powerThe school has no electricity supply of any kind.
5 · Poor classroomsAt least half the rooms are bush material or need major repair.
6 · Gender imbalanceGender parity index below 0.85 at schools with 20 or more pupils.

How is risk spread across the province?

Most schools sit in the middle — one or two flags — but the right-hand tail is long and dangerous. 211 schools carry three or more flags and 17 carry five or more. The four schools failing every test are the sharpest end of the problem.

Which districts carry the most risk?

Nuku carries the most risk on both measures — 74 schools at three or more flags and the highest average score (2.43). Telefomin is the lowest-risk district (average 2.05). Aitape/Lumi and Vanimo/Green River sit close together in the middle, but Vanimo/Green River has the larger raw count of high-risk schools because it has more schools overall.

Mapul Primary — 201 children, 0 teachers

A “ghost school” in Aitape/Lumi: 201 children are enrolled but no teacher is posted. This is a posting failure, not a building problem, and can be fixed with a redeployment letter this term.

What the Board should do next

Three moves follow directly from the index — act on the worst schools, fix the ghost-school posting, and sequence the rest of the support by the flag type that dominates each school.

Visit the top 17 immediately

Send a district officer to every school carrying five or more flags before the 2026 year settles. These 17 schools, led by the four at the full six, are failing on teachers and buildings at once and cannot wait for the normal cycle.

Fix the Mapul posting now

Resolve the Mapul Primary ghost school (201 enrolled, 0 teachers) with a redeployment letter this term. It is the single cheapest fix on this list — no construction, just a teacher posted to children already showing up.

Sequence support by flag type

Group the 211 high-risk schools by their dominant flag: post teachers where the gap is staffing, build toilets and tanks where the gap is water and sanitation, and target female-teacher housing where gender imbalance drives the score.
