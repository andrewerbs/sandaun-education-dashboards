How equal is schooling for girls in Sandaun — and where does that change?

Girls enter school almost in step with boys. By the time the few who remain reach Grade 8, one in five girls’ places has been lost relative to boys. This dashboard tracks the gender parity index (GPI — girls enrolled per boy enrolled, where 1.0 is parity) grade by grade and district by district, and links the pattern to the teaching workforce: who is standing at the front of the classroom.

Girls start near parityPrep GPI is 0.94 province-wide — close to balanced. Girls arrive at school in almost equal numbers to boys.
The gap widens with every gradeGPI slides to 0.79 by Grade 8. The loss is steepest in the upper grades, exactly where overall dropout is highest.
Districts diverge sharplyGrade 8 GPI ranges from 0.87 in Vanimo/Green River down to 0.65 in Telefomin — a wide spread for one province.

Gender parity by grade

Province-wide GPI starts near-balanced in Prep and declines steadily across the grades. There is no single drop — instead a sustained drift downward, accelerating from Grade 6. The line never recovers.

0.94 → 0.79

Province GPI falls from 0.94 in Prep to 0.79 by Grade 8 — a 0.16 loss in girls’ relative presence across nine grades.

Is the decline the same everywhere? No — the four districts diverge

All four districts start close to parity in Prep. They part company in the upper grades. Vanimo/Green River has the highest Grade 8 parity (0.87); Telefomin falls to 0.65 — the lowest Grade 8 gender parity in the province. The teacher mix and the parity gap

Set each district’s share of female teachers beside its Grade 8 GPI and the pattern is plain: where more women teach, more girls stay. Vanimo/Green River (41% female teachers) holds the highest Grade 8 GPI; Telefomin (28% female, the lowest) has the lowest.
