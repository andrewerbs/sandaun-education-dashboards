Reading this dashboard

This page answers one question: of the 89,730 children counted in the 2025 census, where are they, where do we lose them, and how many make it to Grade 8? Enrolment is large at the bottom and collapses through the primary years. The numbers below are built from a single census year — transition and survival are synthetic-cohort estimates, not tracked cohorts.

The shape of the system: a heavy base that narrows fast

How is enrolment distributed across the grades? Prep alone holds 18,100 children — more than a fifth of the whole system — and each grade above it is smaller than the last. The early-grade bulge is real demand: children turn up. The narrowing above Grade 2 is the system failing to hold them.

The base is fullPrep–Grade 2 hold 46,542 children — 52% of all enrolment sits in the first three grades. Demand for schooling is not the problem.
The top is thinGrades 6–8 hold just 19,373 children — 22% of enrolment. By Grade 8 only 6,705 remain, fewer than half the Grade 1 intake.

The Grade 2 → Grade 3 cliff

Where is the sharpest loss? At every grade boundary we ask how many children carry forward. The answer is steady until Grade 2, then it falls off a cliff: only 70.2% of Grade 2 children appear in Grade 3 — the point where most children must move from an elementary school to a separate primary school.

are lost between Grade 2 (12,412) and Grade 3 (8,713) — only 70% make the step. This is the single worst transition in the system.

Survival to Grade 8: only 37% of the Prep class remains

If we follow the Prep intake of 18,100 children up the grades, how many are still enrolled by Grade 8? Roughly 37% — 6,705 children. Almost two in three children who start school have left the system before Grade 8. The steepest part of the decline is the Grade 2–Grade 3 cliff.

18,100 → 6,705

From the Prep base to Grade 8, the system retains roughly 1 child in 3. Holding even 10 more points to Grade 8 would mean about 1,800 additional children completing primary school each year.

The cliff is not the same in every district

Which districts lose the most children at the Grade 2–Grade 3 step? Aitape/Lumi holds 81% of its Grade 2 children into Grade 3 and is the natural benchmark. Nuku is the pinch-point at 64% — the worst transition in the province — while Telefomin and Vanimo/Green River both sit at 69%.

Data note: why one transition reads above 100%

Why does the Grade 7→Grade 8 step show 107.3% when every other step loses children? Because these figures come from a single census year, not a tracked cohort. We compare this year's Grade 7 against this year's Grade 8 — a synthetic cohort. When more children sit in the higher grade than the lower one, the ratio passes 100%. It does not mean the system gained children.

Re-entry and repetitionOlder children returning to school, or repeating Grade 8, inflate the Grade 8 count relative to Grade 7.
In-migration to larger schoolsChildren moving in for upper primary — especially in Vanimo/Green River, where Grade 8 (3,339) exceeds Grade 7 (3,266) — lift the ratio.
Read transitions as a pattern, not a countUse the boundaries to find the worst leaks (the cliff at G2→G3), not to count exact children moving between two specific grades.
Survival is an estimateThe 37% Prep→G8 figure is a cross-sectional ratio (6,705 ÷ 18,100), not a followed class — directionally robust, not exact.

What this means for retention in 2026

The flow data points to one priority above all: hold children across the Grade 2–Grade 3 boundary, starting in Nuku where the loss is worst.

Operations — bridge the cliff

Target the Grade 2→Grade 3 boundary in Nuku (64%) first, then Telefomin and Vanimo/Green River (69%). Map which Grade 2 children have a primary school within reach and post staff to receive them.

Resources — follow the benchmark

Study what Aitape/Lumi does to hold 81% across the cliff and replicate it. Concentrate support on the lower primary grades where the two worst steps (G1→G2, G2→G3) sit back to back.

Performance — report survival

Make Prep→Grade 8 survival (now 37%) and the G2→G3 transition the headline retention measures reported to the Board each year, by district, and watch them move.
