What the 2025 census shows

Sandaun is PNG’s westernmost province, with schools strung from the Vanimo coast across the Sepik plains to the Telefomin highlands. In the 2025 census, children enrol in large numbers in the early grades and enrolment falls steadily through primary; the schools with the steepest losses are also those recording the fewest teachers, houses, and permanent classrooms.

The sharpest drop is between Grade 2 and Grade 370% of Grade 2 children are enrolled in Grade 3 — 3,699 fewer in a single step. 37% of the Prep enrolment is still enrolled at Grade 8.

Enrolment between Grade 2 and Grade 3

Enrolment is large in the early grades and declines through the primary years. The single sharpest drop is at the Grade 2→Grade 3 boundary — the point where most children move from an elementary school to a separate primary school.

3,699 fewer

are enrolled in Grade 3 (8,713) than in Grade 2 (12,412) — 70% make the step, the largest single-grade drop in the system.

The four districts

Aitape/Lumi records the highest transition and the lowest pupil–teacher ratios. Nuku records the largest understaffing and the most bush-material classrooms. Telefomin records the fewest female teachers and the lowest Grade 8 gender parity. Vanimo/Green River enrols the most students and records the highest gender parity.

Gender parity by grade

The gender parity index (girls per boy enrolled) starts near balanced and declines steadily across the grades, reaching 0.79 by Grade 8. In the upper primary grades, where enrolment falls most, girls’ enrolment falls faster than boys’.

Teachers, housing, and facilities

Many schools record several gaps at once — an overloaded or absent teacher, no staff housing, bush-material classrooms, and no clean water. These gaps are concentrated in the same schools that record the steepest enrolment loss.

Pupil–teacher ratioMedian 35, mean 42. 192 schools (37%) exceed 40 pupils per teacher; the most overloaded record a single teacher to 150–250 children.
Staff housing200 schools (40%) have no house at all. The province is short ~950 houses; only 28% of schools meet the one-house-per-teacher standard.
ClassroomsOnly 46% of classrooms are permanent; 26% are bush material. 127 schools teach entirely in bush-material rooms.
Water & power141 schools (27%) have a critical water situation; 241 schools lack both soap and water; 44% have no reliable power.

A school-by-school risk index

Each school scores 0–6 on six risk flags — teacher gap (40+ pupils per teacher or none at all), no usable toilet, critical water, no power, poor classrooms (mostly bush or needing major repair), and a gender imbalance. 211 schools (40%) record three or more flags; 17 record five or more.
